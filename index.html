01 — What Are Nuclear Weapons

Energy that
moves worlds.
And ends them.

Nuclear weapons release immense energy from atomic reactions — either splitting atoms apart (fission) or fusing them together (fusion). The result is an explosion capable of levelling entire cities in seconds. But beyond raw destruction, nuclear weapons are defined by what they represent: the moment humanity gained the power to end itself.

02 — History & Evolution

The Arms Race

1939
The Beginning

1939

Einstein Writes
to Roosevelt

Albert Einstein and Leo Szilard warn President Roosevelt that Nazi Germany may be developing atomic weapons. This letter sets in motion the Manhattan Project — the most ambitious scientific undertaking in history, involving over 130,000 people across three countries.

July 16, 1945

Trinity.
The First Detonation.

In the New Mexico desert, the world's first nuclear device detonates. The explosion equals 21,000 tons of TNT. J. Robert Oppenheimer recalls a line from Hindu scripture: "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds." Humanity has crossed a threshold it can never uncross.

August 6, 1945

Hiroshima
Erased.

At 8:15 AM, "Little Boy" detonates 600 metres above Hiroshima. 70,000 people die instantly. By the end of 1945, the death toll reaches 140,000. Shadows of vaporised human beings are burned into stone steps. Nothing in the history of warfare had ever moved this fast.

1949

The Soviets
Test Joe-1.

The United States loses its nuclear monopoly. The Soviet Union detonates its first atomic bomb, shocking Western intelligence who thought it would take until 1953. The Cold War arms race begins in earnest. Two superpowers now hold the power to destroy each other — and the world.

1962

13 Days
From Annihilation.

Soviet missiles discovered in Cuba. For 13 days, the world holds its breath. A single miscommunication, a single order misread — and the missiles fly. The crisis ends, but reveals how precarious the balance truly is. We came closer to nuclear war than most people know.

Today

12,500
Warheads Remain.

Nine countries now possess nuclear weapons. Despite arms reduction treaties, the global arsenal retains the capacity for civilisation-ending destruction many times over. The weapons have never been used since Nagasaki — but they have never stopped existing either.

01 / 04 — Applications

Mutually
Assured
Destruction

MAD is the doctrine that kept the Cold War cold. If both sides can annihilate each other, neither dares strike first. It is deterrence through existential terror — a balance of power maintained by the shared guarantee of total destruction. Grotesque in theory. Effective in practice.

02 / 04 — Applications

Nuclear
Power
Generation

The same fission reaction that destroyed Hiroshima powers millions of homes. Nuclear power produces enormous energy with near-zero carbon emissions. Today it supplies 10% of the world's electricity. The debate over its safety versus its necessity defines the energy conversation of our century.

03 / 04 — Applications

Medical
Applications

Radiation that kills in war saves lives in medicine. Radiotherapy targets and destroys cancer cells. Nuclear imaging — PET scans, MRI — allows doctors to see inside the human body with extraordinary precision. The same physics that powers bombs has become one of medicine's most powerful tools.

04 / 04 — Applications

Strategic
Deterrence
Today

Modern nuclear strategy goes far beyond MAD. Submarine-launched missiles ensure second-strike capability. Tactical nukes blur the line between conventional and nuclear war. As new powers join the nuclear club and old treaties collapse, the strategic landscape grows more complex — and more dangerous — by the decade.

04 — Implications

The Cost
of Power.

01
Humanitarian Cost

The two bombs dropped on Japan killed between 129,000 and 226,000 people — mostly civilians. Survivors, known as hibakusha, faced radiation sickness, cancers, and social stigma for decades. The humanitarian case against nuclear weapons is built on these lives.

02
Environmental Impact

A large-scale nuclear exchange could trigger "nuclear winter" — soot and debris blocking sunlight for years, causing global crop failure and mass starvation. Even a limited regional nuclear war between India and Pakistan could kill more people through famine than through the blasts themselves.

03
Geopolitical Tension

Nuclear weapons do not just kill — they reshape international order. They grant their possessors a form of immunity from conventional military pressure. Nations that acquire them change their standing in global politics permanently. Proliferation is thus not merely a security issue but a civilisational one.
